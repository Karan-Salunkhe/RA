## 📌 Project Overview

South Carolina faces a **Dual-Peak energy challenge**: extreme humidity-driven cooling loads in summer and resistive heating surges during winter cold snaps. As the residential footprint expands across coastal and upstate regions, existing grid infrastructure is pushed to its thermal limits — and most municipal utility cooperatives are still operating on top-down, aggregate load forecasts that miss the granular "Behind-the-Meter" variables that actually drive demand.

**SmartGrid Insights** addresses this by developing a high-fidelity predictive analytics framework and an interactive **Decision Support System (DSS)** that forecasts residential energy consumption spikes across South Carolina's diverse climate zones. By training XGBoost gradient boosting models on multi-year historical usage, building metadata, and high-resolution meteorological data, the system identifies **"High-Stress Windows"** — time periods where cooling or heating demands threaten grid stability — enabling utility providers to deploy proactive demand-response programs before brownouts occur.

### The Three Core Questions This Project Answers

| Question | Description |
|---|---|
| **The Sensitivity Question** | How much additional MW load is generated by every 1°F increase above the 85°F "Tipping Point"? |
| **The Demographic Question** | Which housing profiles (e.g., 1970s stick-built vs. modern modular) contribute most to the "Non-Linear Surge" during peak hours? |
| **The Mitigation Question** | What is the theoretical "Peak Shave" achievable through targeted smart-thermostat interventions in specific high-density zip codes? |

## 🗃️ Data Sources & Engineering Strategy

The project integrates three distinct data verticals into a unified modeling pipeline:

### 1. Residential Load Profiles *(Primary)*
Hourly interval data (kWh) for 15,000+ simulated and recorded residential units across South Carolina counties, including sub-metered data for cooling and heating components to isolate weather-sensitive load from baseline consumption.

### 2. Meteorological Data *(Environmental)*
Ten years of historical weather records from NOAA stations in Columbia, Charleston, and Greenville. Key engineered features include:

- **Dry Bulb Temperature** — sensible heat load driver
- **Relative Humidity** — critical for South Carolina's "Apparent Temperature" calculations
- **Dew Point** — the strongest single correlate with cooling energy demand in humid climates
- **Global Horizontal Irradiance (GHI)** — solar heat gain through windows (accounts for the afternoon demand surge)
- **Wind Speed** — convective cooling offset for envelope calculations

### 3. Building Metadata *(Structural)*
A comprehensive property characteristics ledger covering:

- **Square Footage & Volume** — baseline cooling/heating capacity required
- **Envelope Quality** — R-values for wall/attic insulation and window glazing type
- **HVAC Specifications** — unit age and SEER (Seasonal Energy Efficiency Ratio) ratings
- **Construction Era** — proxy for insulation standards (1970s stick-built vs. post-2000 modular)
- **Occupancy Profile** — number of residents and typical occupancy hours

### Data Quality & Privacy Standards
- **PII Protection:** All `Building_ID` markers are salted-hash anonymized — no unit can be linked to an individual resident
- **Missing Weather Data:** Forward-filling imputation for gaps ≤ 3 hours; NOAA neighbor-station interpolation for longer outages
- **Sub-metering Gaps:** Median aggregation for sensor latency gaps; flagged in the output schema
- **Vacancy Detection:** Homes with zero usage for 72+ consecutive hours are excluded from peak-load calculations as statistical outliers

## 🧠 Technical Approach

### Modeling Architecture

The primary engine is an **XGBoost (Extreme Gradient Boosting) regressor**, chosen specifically for its ability to capture the **"Hockey-Stick" curve** of energy demand — where consumption stays approximately flat until a temperature threshold (~85°F), then rises non-linearly as compressor systems reach capacity limits.

**Two separate models are trained:**

| Model | Target Variable | R² (Achieved) | Primary Drivers |
|---|---|---|---|
| Cooling Model | Hourly cooling kWh | **0.69** | Dry bulb temp, humidity, GHI, SEER rating |
| Heating Model | Hourly heating kWh | **0.66** | Dry bulb temp, dew point, insulation R-value, unit age |

**Key Feature Engineering decisions:**

- **Lag Features** — temperature from the previous 1, 3, and 6 hours to account for *thermal mass* (a house stays hot hours after outdoor temperature begins to drop)
- **Cooling Degree Days (CDD)** — cumulative heat stress above 65°F base, the industry-standard demand proxy
- **Apparent Temperature** — heat index combining dry bulb and humidity for a "feels like" load signal
- **Hour-of-Day × Day-of-Week interactions** — capture occupancy-driven load patterns (e.g., Friday afternoon pre-cooling)
- **Climate Zone Encoding** — South Carolina spans 3 ASHRAE climate zones; zone is encoded as a categorical feature

**Hyperparameter Tuning:**
Randomized Search CV over `max_depth` (controls overfitting to specific heatwave signatures), `learning_rate`, `n_estimators`, `subsample`, and `colsample_bytree`, with 5-fold cross-validation stratified by month to prevent data leakage between summer and winter seasons.

**Baseline Comparisons:**
XGBoost is benchmarked against OLS Linear Regression and Random Forest. XGBoost outperforms both on RMSE and R² across all three climate zones (Columbia, Charleston, Greenville).

## 🤖 LLM-Augmented Analysis: AI Strategy Panel

The **Gemini 2.5 Flash API** is integrated as an *"Executive Translator"* — converting model outputs into actionable plain-English insights for non-technical utility managers and dispatch operators.

### Three AI Capabilities

**1. Contextual Feature Importance Narration**
Instead of presenting raw SHAP value bar charts, the AI interprets them in operational language:
> *"The model prioritized Dew Point over Dry Bulb Temperature this weekend, suggesting that latent heat — not just heat — is the primary driver of the current 4:00 PM load spike. Demand-response messaging should reference 'humidity relief' rather than temperature thresholds to improve customer participation."*

**2. Proactive Policy Drafting**
The AI scans predicted peaks and auto-generates customer outreach text for utility communications:
> *"Peak Alert: Columbia is forecast to hit 98°F today. Join our 'Cooling Credit' program by setting your AC to 74°F between 2 PM and 6 PM. Participants receive a $15 bill credit."*

**3. Smart Thermostat Prioritization**
Utility managers can query: *"Which 500 homes should we prioritize for the next smart thermostat rollout to maximize load shedding?"* — the AI cross-references model feature importance, building metadata, and geographic density to return a ranked address list with projected MW reduction per cohort.

### AI Safety Guardrails
To prevent dangerous suggestions, the following hard-coded constraints override any LLM output:
- Thermostat suggestions are bounded: **62°F ≤ setpoint ≤ 78°F**
- Load-shedding recommendations cannot exceed **15% of baseline** for any single ZIP code
- All AI-generated policy text is tagged with a confidence tier (`HIGH / MEDIUM / LOW`) derived from the model's prediction interval for the relevant time window

## 🚀 Deliverables: The Grid Optimization Suite

### 1. Predictive Dashboard
Real-time visualization of forecasted load vs. grid capacity for the next **72 hours**, filterable by County and Housing Type. Powered by the XGBoost inference pipeline with a sub-15-second end-to-end latency target (ingest → inference → AI summary).

### 2. "What-If" Simulator
An interactive panel where utility operators slide a **Temperature Offset** (e.g., +5°F) to see the projected impact on grid stability and peak MW demand. Supports scenario modeling for extreme weather events including simulated analogs of the 2014 Polar Vortex and 2022 Heatwave.

### 3. Retrofit Impact Report
A static analytical module ranking the **Return on Investment** for weatherization interventions by county and housing cohort:
> *"Upgrading attic insulation in Richland County reduces peak load by 12% more effectively than HVAC replacements for pre-1980 housing stock."*

### 4. AI Strategy Panel
A natural language interface backed by Gemini 2.5 Flash that allows utility managers to query predicted peaks, generate demand-response outreach, and receive prioritized intervention lists — all grounded in the live model output with hallucination guardrails active.

## ⚠️ Risks & Mitigations

**Data Drift**
The relationship between weather and energy load shifts as residents adopt solar panels, EVs, and battery storage. A **Retraining Trigger** is implemented: if model MAE exceeds 10% for three consecutive operational days, the system flags the data for manual review and initiates a retraining queue.

**AI Hallucination on Safety-Critical Outputs**
LLMs may suggest dangerous load-shedding levels. Hard-coded safety floors override all AI-generated thermostat or shedding recommendations before they reach the user interface, with violations logged for audit.

**Computational Bottlenecks**
Large-scale inference across 15,000 homes in near-real-time. Mitigated via XGBoost's native GPU acceleration and batch-processing AI summaries at the **neighborhood level** rather than individual house level, reducing API call volume by ~97%.

## 🌱 Business Impact & Recommendations

- **Promote energy-efficient technologies** — heat pumps, attic insulation upgrades, and smart thermostats deliver the highest load-reduction ROI for pre-1980 housing stock
- **Implement smart pricing strategies** — time-of-use rates incentivize pre-cooling during morning hours, flattening the 4–6 PM peak
- **Target high-usage households** — the top 10% of consumers account for ~35% of peak load; personalized outreach to this cohort achieves outsized grid impact
- **Support utility planning** — 72-hour forecasted load curves allow dispatch operators to coordinate peaker plant activation 48 hours earlier, reducing both fuel cost and carbon intensity
